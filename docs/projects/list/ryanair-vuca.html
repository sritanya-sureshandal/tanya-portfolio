A strategy project worth its weight is one where the frameworks earn their keep. On Ryanair, PESTLE gave us the macro view, Porter’s gave us the industry view, TOWS linked the two, and VUCA named the weather the airline was flying through.

The brief

Co-author a strategic report analysing the airline industry pre- and post-COVID-19, with a particular focus on how Ryanair leveraged environmental analysis and dynamic capabilities to manage the pandemic and recover profitability.

By 2022 Ryanair had rebuilt to 97 million passengers and €4.8 billion revenue. The question was not whether they recovered but what enabled it - and what that tells us about strategy in genuinely volatile environments.

Methodological frame. PESTLE (external drivers) → Five Forces (industry structure) → SWOT/TOWS (options) → VUCA (how to decide under uncertainty). Each step narrows the question before the one after it is asked.

Porter’s Five Forces, scored

Every framework is only as useful as the discipline it forces. We scored each force on a 1–5 scale (1 weak, 5 strong) pre- and post-COVID:

Code 
forces = pd.DataFrame({
    "Force": [
        "New entrants", "Substitutes", "Buyer power",
        "Supplier power", "Competitive rivalry",
    ],
    "Pre-COVID (2019)":  [3, 3, 4, 4, 5],
    "Post-shock (2022)": [2, 3, 4, 5, 4],
})

fig = go.Figure()
fig.add_trace(go.Scatterpolar(
    r=forces["Pre-COVID (2019)"].tolist()   + [forces["Pre-COVID (2019)"][0]],
    theta=forces["Force"].tolist()          + [forces["Force"][0]],
    fill="toself", name="Pre-COVID (2019)", line_color="#2D5F4E",
    fillcolor="rgba(45, 95, 78, 0.25)",
))
fig.add_trace(go.Scatterpolar(
    r=forces["Post-shock (2022)"].tolist()  + [forces["Post-shock (2022)"][0]],
    theta=forces["Force"].tolist()          + [forces["Force"][0]],
    fill="toself", name="Post-shock (2022)", line_color="#B45309",
    fillcolor="rgba(180, 83, 9, 0.18)",
))
fig.update_layout(
    polar=dict(radialaxis=dict(visible=True, range=[0, 5])),
    height=460, margin=dict(l=40, r=40, t=40, b=40),
    paper_bgcolor="rgba(0,0,0,0)",
)
fig.show()

Notable shift. Supplier power moved from 4 to 5 as post-COVID fuel volatility, Boeing delivery delays, and staffing shortages all compounded. Competitive rivalry softened slightly as Flybe, Alitalia, and Norwegian exited or restructured.

VUCA - naming the weather

Ryanair’s response to VUCA was not a plan - it was a posture. Low cost, fleet simplicity, balance-sheet reserve, and willingness to expand when others retrenched. That posture is what allowed the 2022 recovery to look so sharp in retrospect.

A small calculation on cost resilience

A simple sensitivity: how does Ryanair’s ~€40/passenger cost base compare to a hypothetical competitor at €60/passenger across a demand shock?

Code 
prices = np.arange(30, 121, 5)
ryan_cost = 40
comp_cost = 60

margins = pd.DataFrame({
    "ticket_price": prices,
    "Ryanair_margin": prices - ryan_cost,
    "Competitor_margin": prices - comp_cost,
})

fig = go.Figure()
fig.add_trace(go.Scatter(x=margins["ticket_price"], y=margins["Ryanair_margin"],
                         mode="lines+markers", name="Ryanair (€40 cost)",
                         line=dict(color="#2D5F4E", width=3)))
fig.add_trace(go.Scatter(x=margins["ticket_price"], y=margins["Competitor_margin"],
                         mode="lines+markers", name="Competitor (€60 cost)",
                         line=dict(color="#B45309", width=3, dash="dash")))
fig.add_hline(y=0, line_dash="dot", line_color="#94A3B8")
fig.update_layout(height=420, xaxis_title="Ticket price (€)",
                  yaxis_title="Margin per passenger (€)",
                  margin=dict(l=20, r=20, t=20, b=40),
                  plot_bgcolor="rgba(0,0,0,0)", paper_bgcolor="rgba(0,0,0,0)")
fig.show()

The €20/passenger cost advantage is the headline. Equally important is the breakeven gap: Ryanair continues to clear zero at ticket prices where the competitor is loss-making. In a demand shock, that gap is strategy.

Recovery profitability - the equation that matters

The recovery can be summarised neatly in a standard revenue decomposition:

\[ \text{Revenue} = P \times Q \times L \]

Where \(P\) is average ticket price, \(Q\) is seat capacity deployed, and \(L\) is load factor. Ryanair’s post-shock play was to expand \(Q\) aggressively while competitors could not, accept modest pressure on \(P\), and rely on operational discipline to keep \(L\) above 90 %.

## Recovery profitability - the equation that matters

The recovery can be summarised neatly in a standard revenue decomposition:

$$
\text{Revenue} = P \times Q \times L
$$

Where $P$ is average ticket price, $Q$ is seat capacity deployed, and $L$ is load factor. Ryanair's post-shock play was to **expand $Q$ aggressively** while competitors could not, accept modest pressure on $P$, and rely on operational discipline to keep $L$ above 90 %.
