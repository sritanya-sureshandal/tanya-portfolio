Why I Chose People Over Code

Career
Reflection
I did not leave Computing behind. I brought it with me and put it in service of something bigger - understanding how people and organisations actually work.

Sritanya Suresh Andal

Published

28 September 2025

Essay · September 2025

When people ask why I switched from Computing to Business Analytics, the efficient answer is true. The more honest one is simpler and harder to explain in a sentence.

When people ask why I switched from Computing to Business Analytics, I sometimes give the efficient answer: broader career options, interest in data, the intersection of technology and strategy. All of that is true. But the more honest answer is simpler and harder to explain in a sentence.

I wanted to work with people, not just systems.

What three years of Computing actually taught me

I spent three years studying Computer Applications - building things, debugging things, learning the internal logic of machines. I was good at it. I genuinely enjoyed the problem-solving dimension. But I kept noticing that the problems I found most interesting were not technical problems. They were human ones:

  • Why do organisations make poor decisions despite having good data?
  • Why do teams fail even when everyone is individually competent?
  • What does it actually take to translate an insight into a change that sticks?

Those questions pulled me in a different direction.

The turning point I did not recognise

My year as VP of the Students’ Union was the turning point I did not recognise until later. I was coordinating 70+ events, representing thousands of students, navigating institutional relationships - and none of the skills that mattered most were technical. They were strategic, communicative, and human.

I was doing data-adjacent work constantly: reading patterns in student feedback, assessing what was working, making decisions under incomplete information. But I was doing it in service of people and outcomes, not systems.

The one-week decision

By the time I landed in Dublin, I was enrolled in a Masters in Computing. I switched majors in the first week.

That sounds impulsive. It was not. Something about being in a new country, in a new classroom, looking at the next year ahead of me made it suddenly very clear that I did not want to go further into pure computing. I wanted the intersection - where data meets decision-making, where analysis meets strategy, where the technical and the human have to work together. Business Analytics felt like exactly that. So I made the call, early, before I had time to talk myself out of it.

What I was afraid of - and what I found

Switching came with its own anxiety. I walked into my new programme worried I would be the odd one out: the tech person in a room full of business-fluent peers, my background showing in the wrong ways.

What I found instead is that it showed in the right ones. I can build the models, interrogate the data, ask the questions that get skipped among frameworks.

I did not leave Computing behind. I brought it with me and put it in service of something bigger: understanding how people and organisations actually work, and how good analysis can help them work better.

That is still what drives me.
